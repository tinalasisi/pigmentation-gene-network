A peptide sold on the internet

People inject a hormone analog to get a tan.
Why does that work when no cream ever has?

"Melanotan" — an unapproved synthetic copy of the hormone α‑MSH — is sold online to darken skin evenly, everywhere, on demand. Dermatology has never shipped a cream, pill, or gene therapy that does the same thing by acting on the skin itself. We mapped the entire human pigmentation‑disorder genome to find out why — and found the one cell in the map that is provably, structurally empty.

What fills the empty cell

You can switch human pigment OFF everywhere with a skin gene.
Only a hormone switches it ON everywhere.

Two independent, diffusible endocrine signals — melanocortin (α‑MSH → MC1R) and estrogen (→ GPER1, a verified melanocyte cis‑eGene, q≈0.005; melanocytes are ERα‑negative so this is not the classical estrogen receptor) — funnel into the exact same intracellular node: cAMP → CREB → MITF.

More than one hand on the switch — but only one switch. (The estrogen arm is the mechanistic basis of melasma / pregnancy hyperpigmentation.) Every convergence edge was checked against a live PubMed lookup.

Back to the cold open

Melanotan is the architecture's prediction, sold on the internet.

Melanotan II is a synthetic, non‑selective analog of α‑MSH. It doesn't touch skin — it injects the one whole‑body signal that was always going to work, because the grid shows there is no second route: the melanocortin input into cAMP → CREB → MITF. The FDA‑approved, MC1R‑selective cousin (afamelanotide, marketed once as "Melanotan I") treats a rare light‑sensitivity disorder — same dial, medically controlled.

The twist — molecular evolution, same network, no new data

The drug target is evolutionarily disposable.
The machine it triggers is locked down.

Across 753 scored network genes, synthesis‑floor enzymes (TYR, OCA2, SLC45A2…) and the intracellular MITF–cAMP–CREB dial core (MITF, CREB1, PRKACA, GNAS…) separate completely on LoF tolerance (Mann‑Whitney U=64, p=1.55e‑4, r=−1.00) and tissue breadth (U=0, p=6.75e‑4, r=1.00).

MC1R — the Melanotan target — has LOEUF 1.93, rank 4 of 753 (top 0.5% most LoF‑tolerant), grouped with the disposable floor and POMC, not with the core it activates. MITF itself sits deep in the constrained tail (LOEUF 0.48, pLI 0.992).

Where this stands

One dial, two hands, one disposable trigger.

Honest limits: the mechanism→direction rule ("losing a positive melanin regulator lightens skin") is a methodological check, not a discovery — it is close to the definition of albinism. MFSD12 loss reduces pheomelanin and is associated with darker skin, not lighter. Primate coat‑dichromatism evolution (15 independent origins, always polygenic) is a separate evidence track, not merged into this disorder‑architecture claim.

What did Claude Science help us do

Re‑annotated ~200 disorder genes by direction/spread/cell‑of‑origin, collapsed raw gene counts to real clinical entities (correcting a ~3× GWAS‑swarm inflation), ran 3 adversarial verification passes, and PubMed‑checked every hormone‑convergence edge.

How are we closer to a finding

We didn't just find "genes for pigment" — we found the network has exactly one whole‑body up‑channel, it's endocrine not epidermal, two hormones share it, and its human‑drug target is the most evolutionarily expendable node in the whole pathway.

What is the flagship

The empty quadrant cell (23/0) plus the convergent single dial (cAMP→CREB→MITF) plus its disposable trigger (MC1R, LOEUF 1.93, rank 4/753) — three independently‑verified results that lock together into one causal story, illustrated by a drug already sold on the internet.
