# Bird sexual-dichromatism genetics — grounded mini-review

**Purpose.** The primate result is framed against a bird contrast. The repo currently anchors that
contrast on a single 2007 paper and calls birds a "single-gene MC1R switch." That framing is a
straw man the literature refutes. This note pins the accurate state of the field to verified
sources so every doc edit cites something real. All five references below were retrieved and
verified via PubMed (PMID, DOI, journal, year confirmed from article metadata; abstracts read).

## The five load-bearing sources (verified)

1. **Nadeau et al. 2007** — "Evolution of an avian pigmentation gene correlates with a measure of
   sexual selection." *Proc Biol Sci* 2007. PMID 17504743 · doi:10.1098/rspb.2007.0174 · PMC2270924.
   - What it ACTUALLY claims (from the abstract): a correlation, **in galliform birds**, between the
     rate of amino-acid change (dN/dS) at **MC1R** and the degree of sexual dichromatism, with no
     similar pattern at five other loci. It is a **selection-signal correlation on one melanin gene
     in one order** — explicitly *not* "MC1R builds dichromatism across birds," and not a
     mechanism/knock-in result.

2. **Gazda et al. 2020** — "A genetic mechanism for sexual dichromatism in birds." *Science* 2020.
   PMID 32527835 · doi:10.1126/science.aba0803.
   - Carotenoid-based dichromatism in mosaic canaries is controlled by **BCO2** (β-carotene
     oxygenase 2), via differential carotenoid *degradation* in the integument. Transcriptomics
     suggest carotenoid degradation is "a common mechanism contributing to sexual dichromatism
     across finches." Conclusion in their own words: dichromatism "can evolve through simple
     molecular mechanisms controlled by **genes of major effect**." A SECOND genetic axis
     (carotenoid, not melanin), in a different clade (finches, not galliforms).

3. **Avian coloration genetics review 2021** — "Avian Coloration Genetics: Recent Advances and
   Emerging Questions." *J Hered* 2021. PMID 34002228 · doi:10.1093/jhered/esab015.
   - States the field of avian coloration genetics is "burgeoning," spanning multiple pigment
     classes (carotenoids, psittacofulvins) plus structural color, with color often controlled by
     co-expressed/co-regulated gene sets. Direct evidence the "one gene" framing is obsolete.

4. **Griffith, Falk & Kimball 2026** — "Hormonal Influences on Sexual Dichromatism Across the
   Avian Tree of Life: A Primer." *Integr Comp Biol* 2026. PMID 42262725 · doi:10.1093/icb/icag079.
   - Reviews estrogen/testosterone (and cortisol/thyroid) control of plumage dichromatism and
     reconstructs ancestral states across **>8,800 bird species**. States the physiological/genetic
     mechanisms of dichromatism are "highly diverse across the avian clade." Directly supports the
     **pigment × sex-hormone coupling** thesis as a cross-taxon theme — birds already show it.

5. **Sex-linked genomic variation 2015** — "Sex-linked genomic variation and its relationship to
   avian plumage dichromatism and sexual selection." *BMC Evol Biol* 2015. PMID 26377432 ·
   doi:10.1186/s12862-015-0480-4 · PMC4574164.
   - Adds a genomic-architecture / sex-linkage dimension beyond single candidate loci.

## The corrected framing (what the docs should say)

**WRONG (current straw man):** "In birds, dichromatism is nearly a single-gene MC1R switch."

**RIGHT (accurate, and a stronger contrast):** In birds, the best-studied cases of sexual
dichromatism map to **genes of major effect for a specific pigment system in a specific clade** —
MC1R for melanin-based dichromatism in **galliforms** (Nadeau 2007, a dN/dS–dichromatism
correlation), and BCO2 for carotenoid-based dichromatism in **finches** (Gazda 2020, *Science*). And
these are **hormonally regulated** — the avian dichromatism literature has an active
estrogen/testosterone-control strand (2026 primer). We asked whether the same pigment × sex-hormone
coupling, tested across primates, likewise concentrates on a gene of major effect, and found it is
instead **polygenic, with no single MC1R- or BCO2-analog** carrying the trait — though selection
touches the same coupled pigment–hormone network.

## Why this HELPS the pitch (not just a fix)

- **Removes a straw-man vulnerability.** A judge who knows avian coloration cannot rebut us with
  "there's a 2020 Science paper" — we cite it ourselves.
- **Sharpens the contrast.** "Polygenic, no gene of major effect" is a *specific, defensible*
  contrast to two named avian genes-of-major-effect, far better than "birds simple / primates
  complex."
- **Makes the coupling cross-taxon.** BCO2 is estrogen-responsive and there is a whole avian
  hormone-control literature — so the pigment × sex-hormone coupling is not a primate-only
  invention we assert, it is a theme birds already display. That is a much stronger thesis.
- **A real convergence to claim (optional).** The primate finding "dichromatism lost more readily
  than gained" (~9.1×) can be checked against the avian ancestral-state literature (2026 primer
  reconstructs states across 8,800 species) as a cross-taxon direction, rather than pretending
  birds are the simple foil.
